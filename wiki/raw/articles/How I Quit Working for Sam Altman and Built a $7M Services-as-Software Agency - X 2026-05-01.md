## Full Article

I spent two years working at Sam Altman's company before I quit. It was one of the best business decisions I've ever made.

I got hired at Worldcoin in 2022. After two years of seeing what the corporate grind actually looks like, I decided to do things my own way. Four years later, ColdIQ is at $7M+ ARR. 70 clients. 30+ people across 10 countries. Bootstrapped, no VC, no angel round, no pitch deck. I work wherever I want and the only person I report to is myself.

What I ended up building looks like an agency on the contract and runs like a software business underneath. We call it services-as-software, and the version of that distinction I'll explain at the end is the entire reason the math works. I want to start where the story actually started, because the lessons matter in the order they happened.

## The two years inside Worldcoin

For people who don't know, Worldcoin is Sam's crypto company. The thesis was a physical device called the orb that scans your iris to give you a world ID. As AI gets indistinguishable from humans online, you'd want cryptographic proof that you're a real human on the internet and not a bot. Sam's read was that this problem only gets bigger as AI advances. Worldcoin was his answer to it. It was one of the fastest-growing startups in the world, and on day one I was all in.

At least, that's what I thought. The reality of corporate life hit fast. The hours were long. I was traveling constantly to launch in different European countries. Every single decision I made had to clear someone above me. The thing that really made me hate the job was my manager. When something went wrong, I was thrown under the bus. When something went right, he took the credit. The feeling of being powerless, of having your wins taken from you, is a specific kind of frustration I wouldn't wish on anyone.

On top of that, I was earning €80,000 a year in Germany. Top 10% of earners in the country. If I asked for a promotion, the most I could realistically get was a 4% bump, and that's assuming I got the raise at all. The €80,000 was good money. The frustrating part was the ceiling on how that number could grow, year after year, with someone above me deciding what my work was worth.

I hit a wall, and I still remember the exact moment it clicked into something I could act on instead of something I was just stewing in. I told myself I would never let someone above me decide what I could and couldn't do. I turned that frustration into the fuel to build something where I was the only person who could limit my own growth.

## How I joined Mich and got the first clients

Around the same time, my friend Michel had already started his own agency. He had a website and four clients. I started learning from him, and we ran our first joint engagement together: one of my best friends, Nicholas, runs a recruitment agency and needed help with his outbound. The collaboration worked. Mich and I joined forces officially, and from there everything changed.

The first stage of an agency is the one that kills most of them before they get off the ground. You have no case studies, no testimonials, and no track record to point at. Cold-reaching businesses with zero proof you can deliver is a chicken-and-egg problem. You need clients to build social proof and you need social proof to get clients. If you don't solve it intentionally, you'll spend months with nobody taking your calls.

This is where the playbook starts.

## Playbook 1: How to land clients with zero proof

You solve the proof problem on two fronts at the same time. Risk on one side, trust on the other. Get one without the other and the strategy collapses.

**Front 1: Drop the price of saying yes until skeptics can't refuse.**

Rules to follow:

- **Anchor every guarantee to something measurable.** Vague phrasing like "we'll try our best" falls flat with skeptical buyers. Specific commitments like "if we don't hit X by this date, you pay nothing" are what make the offer feel real to a prospect who has been burned before.

- **Use free deliverables as the conversation starter.** Free lead lists, free audits, free sample workflows. Whatever costs you the least and signals competence the most.

- **Time-box every guarantee.** Open-ended phrasing tends to read as desperation. A 30-day or 60-day window tied to a defined outcome is what flips the tone toward confidence.

- **Refuse to compete on price alone.** A guarantee is a risk-reversal tool. Keep your retainer floor at the level your delivery economics demand and let the guarantee do the work.

**Front 2: Build trust in public so the prospect already knows you before the cold reach lands.**

Rules to follow:

- **Pick one platform and post daily for 6 months minimum.** For us it was LinkedIn. The compounding only starts once you've shipped consistently long enough for the platform to learn you.

- **Share the actual workflow.** Frameworks, screenshots of real campaigns, the exact playbook you used last week with a client. Tactical specificity beats inspiration every time.

- **Plan for the viral post; don't wait for it.** One viral post is the realistic goal of consistent posting. Mich shipped one that produced our first real wave of inbound. That single post turned strangers into leads who already felt they knew us.

- **Search yourself the way a prospect would.** If they Google your name and land on nothing, the guarantee in your cold email gets discounted to zero. You're still a stranger.

The combination is what cracks the wall. Risk reversal in isolation gets you the meeting. Daily content in isolation gets you the follower. The two stacked together produce a paying client who arrives already half-sold on you.

## Playbook 2: The first-three-hires sequence

Two founders can do everything for a while, until they can't. Delivery quality slips, response times drag, and the trust you spent a year building starts leaking from the wrong end. The reflex is to hire fast. Resist it. Your first three hires set the standard every later hire recalibrates against, and getting those three right is what gives you a $7M business instead of a $1M one.

The hiring rule, non-negotiable:

- **Only A players.** People who can own a domain end-to-end, manage their own time, and deliver without being micromanaged.

- **No exceptions for "nice to have" hires.** If the candidate would need significant management to function, the cost of the wrong hire is higher than the cost of waiting another month for the right one.

- **Hire slowly when growth is screaming at you to hire fast.** That feeling of "leaving money on the table" is the trap. A wrong early hire damages the business actively, taking work that should compound and turning it into work that breaks.

What to look for in the first three:

- **Hire 1: A delivery operator** who can own multiple client accounts after a short shadowing period. Their job is execution and quality. Strategy stays with you.

- **Hire 2: A second delivery operator** of the same caliber. Two operators force you to write down what you do, because you can no longer rely on tribal knowledge passing across one person.

- **Hire 3: A delivery lead** who manages the first two and owns quality across the function. This is the role you, the founder, were doing in the first six months.

Hold off on hiring a marketer, an account executive, or a head of operations until the delivery layer can run without you. Every non-delivery hire before that point is friction at best and burn at worst. We scaled to $7M in three years on the back of this rule. There is no version of that growth that survives if we'd hired the wrong first three.

## Playbook 3: The churn defense at scale

Once the team is in place, you assume the messy part is over. It isn't. The next problem is churn, and almost no agency owner runs the math honestly until they're already inside it.

Run the math before you need to:

- At 10 clients with 10% monthly churn, you replace 1 client a month. Manageable.

- At 30 clients, you replace 3 a month just to stay flat.

- At 50 clients, 5 replacements a month.

- At 70 clients (where ColdIQ is now), 7 replacements every month. That is the workload of a full-time closer running at quota who contributes zero to net new growth. They're treadmilling so the rest of the business can catch its breath.

You cannot out-sell churn at scale. You attack it on three fronts simultaneously.

**Front 1: Promise only what you are 99% sure you can deliver.**

- Stop pitching outcomes you are 60-70% sure of, even when the prospect is asking for them. Walk away from the deal sooner.

- Re-anchor every commitment as an under-promise. The conservative target goes in the proposal, and the over-delivery shows up in the QBR.

- Track the gap between what you promised and what you delivered, monthly, per account. The instant that ratio flips, churn moves up weeks before cancellations show up in the dashboard.

**Front 2: Own the meeting cadence and never lose control of the room.**

- Walk into every client meeting with the next plan already in front of you. The moment a client senses you don't know what to do next, the trust is already gone, and nothing recovers it.

- Set a fixed weekly or bi-weekly cadence per account. Skipping a check-in is a churn signal you create yourself.

- Treat speed as the deliverable. Speed is trust in an agency. The faster you respond and the faster you ship, the longer clients stay.

- Send a written recap within 24 hours of every meeting. The client should never have to ask "what did we agree on?"

**Front 3: Add a revenue layer that doesn't depend on more delivery headcount.**

This is where ColdIQ stopped being a normal agency. We launched our cold accelerator. The accelerator is structural revenue we built on purpose, with its own scaling logic. It scales with content. When the agency has a rough month on churn, the accelerator margin keeps the overall business healthy. Margin compounds at the content layer, and that compounding is what carries the business across the cyclicality of services.

## Playbook 4: What to document so the business survives without you

The last thing we did is the part most agencies never even get to. Nothing in the business should depend on a single person showing up. The agency has to run when the A players are out, when a founder is sick, when a key teammate resigns.

What to document, by format:

- **Workflow files in Markdown.** One file per repeatable task: onboarding a new client, running a deliverability audit, escalating a billing dispute. Written for someone who started Monday with zero context.

- **Loom videos for anything visual.** Cursor-driven work, dashboard navigation, anything that loses meaning in text. Title each Loom with the task and the date.

- **Decision logs per client.** Every "why did we do it this way" note, dated and tied to the client. This is what protects you when staff turns over.

- **Escalation protocols for failure modes.** What happens when deliverability tanks. What happens when a client misses a kickoff. What happens when an A player resigns mid-engagement.

Once the workflows are written, automate the parts that don't need a human in the loop. With Claude Code now, internal tools that used to cost tens of thousands of dollars to build are weekend projects we ship ourselves. The same workflow that used to require real headcount now runs in the background. We move as fast at 70 clients today as we did at a fraction of that scale.

Automation is the byproduct here. The point is that no person in the business is structurally required for the business to keep running. That is the version of an agency that survives a bad week, a sick founder, or a key resignation, and it is the version most agencies never reach because they spend all their energy treadmilling at the churn wall.

## Why I call it a services-as-software business

The contract still says services. The infrastructure underneath runs like software. The accelerator is the layer on top of both, and its margin compounds with content.

That is the shape of the agency I think survives the next few years. Pricing pressure on hour-based work is going to compress every operator who hasn't built infrastructure underneath the human layer. Tasks an LLM can do for twenty dollars are going to get priced like that, and the agencies still selling them by the hour are going to have a hard time. The work that holds its value is judgment, relationship, taste, and accountability. Those are the things a human still gets paid for. Everything else should run on its own underneath.

I'm four years in and we've changed the shape of the business three times to get here. We'll change it again. The work I'm doing this year already looks different from the work that built the first $7M, and the work next year will look different again. That's the trade you make when you decide to run an agency as software underneath. The math compounds, and so does the rate at which you have to keep rebuilding the thing.

If you're sitting at a corporate job with a 4% raise ceiling, or running an agency that's stopped scaling at a number you don't want to live at, the playbook is the one I just walked you through. Land clients with risk reversal and content. Hire the first three carefully. Defend churn before you need to. Document everything until the business runs without you in the room. Then build the layer on top that compounds with content.
